At 6:29 AM on October 7, 2023, approximately 6,000 Hamas operatives breached the Israeli border at 119 points along the Gaza perimeter. Within minutes, an elite Nukhba force reached Re'im Base — the Gaza Division's headquarters — and destroyed its communication infrastructure. For the Israeli security establishment, the result was not merely a surprise attack but a systemic collapse: the command-and-control architecture that was supposed to coordinate the response ceased to function at the moment it was needed most.

What happened next defied the expectations of both the attackers and the existing crisis management literature. Thousands of security personnel — conscripts, reservists, police officers, Shin Bet operatives, and civilian emergency response teams — converged on the Gaza border without receiving orders, without functioning communications, and in many cases without weapons. Within hours, these personnel had halted the penetration, regained control of most civilian communities, and prevented the attackers from deepening their advance into Israeli territory. They did so not through centralized command but through decentralized initiative: individuals and ad hoc teams identifying objectives, self-deploying, and coordinating on the ground with personnel they had never previously served alongside.

This paper asks how such an effective operational response was possible under conditions of total command-and-control failure. Existing frameworks for understanding crisis response do not adequately answer this question. The crisis governance literature assumes that effective response depends on the functioning of centralized coordination systems — communication networks, command structures, and interagency protocols (Boin et al., 2016; Comfort et al., 2010). High-reliability organizing theory examines how organizations sustain performance under uncertainty, but presupposes organizational integrity: the principles of mindful organizing require that the organization itself remain functional (Weick & Sutcliffe, 2007). Latent organizing theory describes how dormant structures activate during emergencies (Van Fenema & Romme, 2020), but does not specify which individual-level attributes predict activation or explain how coordination emerges among personnel from different organizations. In short, the literature explains how institutions prepare for crises and how leaders manage them — but not how frontline personnel sustain coordinated action when the institutions and leaders are themselves incapacitated.

To address this gap, we introduce the concept of **Embedded Response Capacity (ERC)**: the set of structural, cultural, and operational capabilities that enable frontline units to mobilize, coordinate, and act effectively when formal command-and-control systems are degraded or disabled. ERC integrates three mechanisms identified in the organizational resilience literature — latent organizing structures that enable rapid mobilization, high-reliability principles that allow authority to migrate toward operational expertise, and shared professional repertoires that permit improvised coordination — and treats their interaction as the source of decentralized crisis performance.

We test the ERC framework empirically against the October 7 case using two original datasets. The first records 369 security personnel killed during the attack, coded by unit, branch, rank, special operations status, service type, and — critically — whether each individual self-deployed or was stationed at their post when the attack began. The second maps 37 combat locations, documenting which units responded at each site, the timing of engagement, and outcomes including fatalities and abductions. Casualty data serve as a proxy for operational engagement: because the presence of a killed individual at a specific location and time is confirmed by the circumstances of death, the dataset permits reconstruction of mobilization patterns that classified deployment records do not capture.

Five hypotheses operationalize the ERC framework. First, we test whether the response exhibited a **two-wave temporal structure** — stationed personnel engaging first, followed by self-deployed personnel — consistent with latent organizing activation (H1). Second, we examine whether **voluntary mobilization** constituted a substantial share of the overall response, rather than being marginal (H2). Third, we test whether specific individual attributes — special operations training, reserves status, and officer rank — **predict self-deployment**, identifying the profile of personnel most likely to exercise initiative under systemic disruption (H3). Fourth, we assess whether attack sites attracted **multi-branch ad hoc teams** composed of personnel from organizationally diverse units, indicating emergent coordination across institutional boundaries (H4). Fifth, we evaluate whether the **presence of a military response** at a given location was associated with reduced kidnapping outcomes, providing a preliminary test of ERC's operational effectiveness (H5).

The results support the ERC framework. The response followed a clear two-wave pattern, with self-deployed personnel arriving a mean of 2.16 hours after stationed personnel (p < .001). Voluntary mobilization was not marginal: 40.8% of all fatalities were self-deployed personnel who joined without orders. A logistic regression model identifies special operations membership (OR = 14.06), reserves status (OR = 13.48), and officer rank (OR = 3.11) as the strongest predictors of self-deployment. Over half of all locations attracted responders from two or more organizational branches, with a mean of 7.6 distinct units per site. And locations that received a military response experienced kidnapping ratios between 3.6% and 18.5%, compared to 59.8% at Nir Oz — the one community that received no organized response.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it advances organizational resilience theory by shifting attention from institutional architecture to **embedded frontline capabilities** as a source of crisis performance — an analytical move that complements rather than replaces the existing focus on institutional design. Second, it introduces a **casualty-based methodological approach** for reconstructing force mobilization patterns in chaotic environments where conventional operational records are incomplete or unavailable. Third, it offers **policy-relevant evidence** that reserve systems and conscript armies — often evaluated as financial liabilities — function as operational resilience assets in the context of swarm warfare and hybrid terrorism, a finding with implications for force structure decisions in democracies facing unconventional security threats.
