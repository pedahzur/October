# Literature Overview: Positioning ERC

## The gap

Existing crisis management and organizational resilience literature focuses on:
1. **Institutional design** — how organizations prepare for crises (HRO, resilience engineering)
2. **Leadership during crisis** — decision-making by senior officials (Boin et al.)
3. **Post-crisis learning** — after-action adaptation

What is **missing**: how frontline personnel sustain coordinated action when centralized systems fail entirely. Most HRO and crisis governance frameworks assume degraded but functional C2. October 7 represents a case of **total C2 collapse** — a more extreme test.

## Key literatures to engage

### High-Reliability Organizing (HRO)
- Weick & Sutcliffe (2007): mindfulness, deference to expertise, reluctance to simplify
- Roberts (1990): HRO in nuclear carriers
- **ERC contribution**: HRO assumes organizational integrity; ERC addresses what happens when the organization itself is disrupted

### Latent organizing
- Majchrzak et al. (2007): dormant coordination structures activated in emergencies
- **ERC contribution**: ERC specifies which individual-level attributes predict activation

### Organizational improvisation
- Ciborra (1996): improvisation as bricolage
- Bechky & Okhuysen (2011): role-based coordination under disruption
- **ERC contribution**: ERC provides empirical evidence for which personnel improvise and how

### Crisis governance
- Boin, Hart, Stern & Sundelius (2005): political dimensions of crisis leadership
- Comfort (2007): self-organization in complex adaptive systems
- **ERC contribution**: shifts focus from top-level leadership to embedded frontline capacity

### Military sociology and reserves
- Cohen (2008): Israel's citizen army
- Peri (2006): civil-military relations in Israel
- **ERC contribution**: reframes reserves as an operational resilience asset, not just a mobilization structure

## Positioning the argument

"Existing frameworks for understanding crisis response assume that some degree of centralized coordination persists. The October 7 case violates this assumption: for several hours, no effective central command existed. The ERC framework explains how effective response emerged from the bottom up — through capabilities embedded in individual training, institutional culture, and operational experience."
