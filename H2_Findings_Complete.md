### Decentralized Mobilization

The second hypothesis predicted that a substantial portion of the responding forces joined the fighting independently rather than being formally deployed in the operational sector at the onset of the attack. Analysis of the fatalities dataset strongly supports this prediction.

Of the 368 fatalities for which mode of joining was coded, 150 (40.8%; 95% CI [35.9%, 45.9%]) were classified as self-joining responders — individuals who were not initially stationed along the Gaza border but arrived independently after learning about the attack. The remaining 218 (59.2%) were stationed personnel present in the operational sector as part of routine deployment. A binomial test confirms that the self-joining proportion significantly exceeds one-third of the responding force (p = .001), establishing that decentralized mobilization was not a marginal phenomenon but a substantial component of the defensive response. When the analysis is restricted to fatalities in combat roles (n = 343, excluding non-combat personnel such as logistics and communications staff), the self-joining rate rises to 42.3%.

The geographic reach of self-joining was extensive. Fatalities from self-joining responders were recorded at 31 of 40 documented locations (77.5%), indicating that decentralized mobilization was not confined to a single sector or a small number of high-profile sites but extended across the full breadth of the battlespace. At several locations — including Be'eri (81.8%), Ofakim (87.5%), Kfar Aza (64.3%), Sderot (57.1%), and Re'eim (57.1%) — self-joining responders constituted a majority of all fatalities, suggesting that the defense of these communities depended substantially on personnel who arrived voluntarily rather than as part of a pre-planned deployment.

The rate of self-joining varied significantly across organizational branches (χ²(3) = 106.0, p < .001, Cramér's V = .54). All 43 Emergency Response Team fatalities were self-joined by definition, as these local civilian defense teams are composed of community members who mobilize from their homes. Among the Shin Bet, 85.7% of fatalities (6 of 7) were self-joining. Among the police, 62.1% (36 of 58) self-joined — reflecting the fact that many officers deployed to the Gaza Envelope from police stations and posts outside the immediate combat zone. Among the military, the rate was lower but still substantial: 25.0% (65 of 260) of IDF fatalities were from self-joining responders, a notable figure given that the IDF maintained standing forces along the border who would have constituted the bulk of the stationed group.

Self-joining rates also differed sharply by service status (χ²(2) = 152.5, p < .001, Cramér's V = .64). The pattern is striking: 93.0% of reservists who were killed (53 of 57) had self-joined, compared to 57.2% of professional service members (83 of 145) and only 8.4% of conscripts (14 of 166). This gradient directly reflects the institutional logic of the Israeli personnel system. Conscripts were overwhelmingly stationed along the border as part of their routine service and were caught in place by the attack. Reservists, by contrast, were by definition not on active duty; their near-universal classification as self-joiners confirms that they mobilized independently — in many cases traveling from civilian residences across the country — without waiting for formal call-up orders. Professional service members occupied an intermediate position, with some stationed in the sector and others deploying from bases, headquarters, or homes elsewhere.

Two characteristics associated with Embedded Response Capacity showed particularly elevated self-joining rates. Among personnel from special operations forces (SOF = 1), 83.3% self-joined (50 of 60), compared to 32.5% among non-SOF personnel (100 of 308) — an odds ratio of 10.40 (χ²(1) = 51.7, p < .001). Among officers, 63.8% self-joined (60 of 94), compared to 32.8% among non-officers (90 of 274) — an odds ratio of 3.61 (χ²(1) = 26.6, p < .001). These disparities indicate that the individuals who were most likely to self-deploy were precisely those who possessed the training, operational experience, and decision-making autonomy theorized as the foundations of Embedded Response Capacity. These findings provide strong empirical support for **H2**, the Voluntary Mobilization Hypothesis. Decentralized initiative was not a marginal supplement to the formal deployment of stationed forces; it constituted more than two-fifths of the security personnel who lost their lives defending the Gaza Envelope on October 7 and 8. The institutional pattern of self-joining — concentrated among reservists, SOF personnel, officers, and police — is consistent with the theoretical expectation that decentralized mobilization draws on the distributed operational competence embedded within the Israeli security system.
