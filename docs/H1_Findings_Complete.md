### Temporal Structure of the Response

The first hypothesis proposed that the Israeli response unfolded in two distinct phases: an initial wave composed of personnel already stationed in the combat zone, followed by a second wave of responders who joined the fighting independently after the attack began. Temporal analysis of the fatalities dataset provides strong support for this expectation.

Of the 369 security personnel fatalities documented in the dataset, 364 could be coded for both time of death and mode of joining. Among these, 216 (59.3%) were classified as stationed — present in the operational sector as part of routine deployment when the attack began — and 148 (40.7%) were classified as self-joined, having arrived independently after learning about the attack or engaged without explicit operational orders.

The two groups exhibit distinct temporal profiles. Stationed personnel experienced earlier casualties, with a mean time to death of 4.05 hours after the onset of the attack (SD = 3.49, median = 3 hours, IQR = 2–5). By contrast, self-joining responders recorded a mean time to death of 6.21 hours (SD = 6.04, median = 4 hours, IQR = 3–7). The difference of 2.16 hours between the group means is statistically significant by both parametric and nonparametric tests (Welch's t = −3.93, p < .001; Mann-Whitney U = 11,143, p < .001). The effect size is moderate (Cohen's d = 0.46; rank-biserial r = 0.30). A Kolmogorov-Smirnov test further confirms that the two distributions differ significantly (D = 0.258, p < .001), indicating that the temporal patterns of the two groups are drawn from distinct underlying distributions rather than representing random variation within a single population.

The two-wave pattern is particularly visible in the early hours of the response. Within the first three hours after the attack began, 67.6% of stationed personnel fatalities had already occurred, compared to only 45.9% of self-joining responders (χ²(1) = 16.10, p < .001). This disparity reflects the immediate exposure of stationed personnel to the initial assault and the temporal lag inherent in decentralized mobilization — the time required for information about the attack to spread, for individuals to make the decision to deploy, and for them to travel to the combat zone from locations outside the immediate sector.

The cumulative distribution curves (Figure 3) illustrate this pattern graphically. The stationed curve rises steeply in the first two to three hours and begins to plateau by hour five, reflecting the concentrated early engagement and gradual attrition of initially deployed forces. The self-joined curve rises more gradually, overtaking the stationed curve's rate of accumulation in the mid-hours (4–9 hours after onset) and continuing to accumulate casualties at a higher rate through the later stages of the fighting. The shaded area between the two curves represents the temporal gap between the first and second waves — the period during which decentralized mobilization was actively augmenting the defensive effort.

These patterns support the existence of two analytically distinguishable waves of responders: stationed personnel who absorbed the initial shock of the attack during the first two to three hours, and self-joining responders who arrived subsequently and reinforced defensive efforts during the middle and later stages of the engagement. The temporal gap between the two waves — approximately two hours at the mean and one hour at the median — corresponds to the time required for decentralized mobilization to generate a second line of response. Notably, the overlap between the two distributions during hours three through five indicates that the transition between waves was gradual rather than discrete: the second wave began arriving while the first wave was still actively engaged.

These findings provide empirical support for **H1**, the Two-Wave Response Hypothesis.
