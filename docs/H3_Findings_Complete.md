### Characteristics of Self-Joining Responders

The third hypothesis proposed that individuals who joined the fighting independently would be more likely to possess characteristics associated with Embedded Response Capacity — specifically, officer status, membership in specialized units, and reserve service. A logistic regression analysis was conducted to test this prediction by examining which individual-level characteristics predict self-joining behavior when all predictors are considered simultaneously.

The analysis was restricted to the 325 fatalities from the military, police, and Shin Bet for whom all predictor variables were coded. The 43 Emergency Response Team fatalities were excluded from the regression because all members self-joined by definition, producing perfect separation on the dependent variable; their contribution is reported separately. The dependent variable is a binary indicator distinguishing self-joining responders (1) from stationed personnel (0). The independent variables are officer status, SOF membership, service status (reserves and professional service, with conscript as the reference category), and organizational branch (police/Shin Bet, with military as the reference).

The full model is statistically significant (likelihood ratio χ² = 153.0, p < .001) and explains a substantial share of variance in self-joining behavior (McFadden pseudo-R² = .371). The model correctly classifies 82.5% of cases, with 93.1% specificity (correctly identifying stationed personnel) and 60.7% sensitivity (correctly identifying self-joiners). Table 4 reports the full regression results.

Four of the five predictors are statistically significant, and each aligns with the theoretical expectations of the ERC framework.

**SOF membership is the strongest predictor.** Controlling for all other variables, membership in a special operations or elite unit increases the odds of self-joining by a factor of 14.06 (p < .001). This finding indicates that the decentralized mobilization observed on October 7 was not a random process: it was disproportionately driven by personnel from units that cultivate the highest levels of tactical competence, initiative, and independent decision-making — precisely the qualities theorized as the core of Embedded Response Capacity. That the effect remains this large after controlling for officer status and service type confirms that SOF membership captures something distinct from rank or employment category: it reflects a specific training culture and operational mindset that predisposes individuals to act without orders.

**Reserve status has a comparably large effect.** Reservists were 13.48 times more likely to self-join than conscripts (p < .001). This reflects the structural logic of the Israeli personnel system: reservists were by definition not on active duty and therefore not stationed in the combat zone. Their presence among the fatalities is itself evidence of voluntary mobilization. But the size of the effect — even after controlling for other characteristics — suggests that reserve service embeds a form of latent readiness: these individuals retained the tactical skills, organizational knowledge, and personal initiative necessary to deploy independently under crisis conditions, often years after completing their regular service.

**Police and Shin Bet personnel** were 7.04 times more likely to self-join than military personnel (p < .001). This reflects the fact that police and intelligence operatives stationed outside the immediate Gaza border sector chose to deploy toward the combat zone on their own initiative, while the military's stationed forces were concentrated along the border itself.

**Officer status** remains significant (OR = 3.11, p = .019) even after controlling for SOF membership and service status, indicating that the training and decision-making autonomy associated with command positions independently contributes to the propensity for decentralized initiative.

**Professional service** (vs. conscript) is not significant in the multivariate model (OR = 1.84, p = .295), despite being highly significant in the bivariate analysis (OR = 7.46, p < .001). This attenuation reflects the substantial overlap between professional service and the other predictors: many SOF personnel and police officers are professional service members, and the multivariate model attributes the self-joining behavior to those more specific institutional characteristics rather than to employment status per se.

An interaction term between SOF and officer status was tested but was not significant (p = .71), indicating that the effects of SOF membership and officer status on self-joining are additive rather than synergistic.

The predicted probabilities derived from the model (Figure 6) illustrate the cumulative effect of ERC-associated characteristics. A conscript, non-officer, non-SOF member of a military unit — the profile with the lowest predicted probability — has only a 7.1% probability of self-joining. By contrast, a reservist who is also an officer and SOF member has a predicted probability of 97.8%. The gradient between these extremes maps directly onto the institutional characteristics theorized as generating Embedded Response Capacity: as training intensity, operational experience, and decision-making autonomy increase, so does the likelihood of voluntary mobilization.

**Emergency Response Teams.** Although excluded from the regression due to perfect separation, the 43 Emergency Response Team fatalities warrant separate discussion. All 43 self-joined, a rate of 100%. These individuals — community-based civilian defense coordinators and first responders — represent the most localized form of Embedded Response Capacity: ordinary citizens whose training and assigned roles within the community defense structure activated immediately upon the onset of the attack. Their universal self-joining is consistent with the theoretical expectation that even modest levels of institutionalized training can generate decentralized initiative under crisis conditions.

These findings provide strong support for **H3**, the ERC Profile Hypothesis. The characteristics that predict self-joining behavior are precisely those associated with the institutional foundations of Embedded Response Capacity: specialized tactical training (SOF), distributed military expertise (reserves), decision-making autonomy (officer status), and interorganizational deployment (police/security services). The multivariate analysis confirms that these effects are independent of one another, indicating that each institutional channel contributes separately to the generation of decentralized initiative.
