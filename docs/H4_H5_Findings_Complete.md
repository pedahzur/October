### Ad Hoc Team Formation

The fourth hypothesis focused on the formation of temporary teams composed of personnel from different organizations. If Embedded Response Capacity allows individuals with shared training and doctrine to coordinate without formal planning, then combat locations should exhibit evidence of cross-organizational cooperation — the simultaneous presence of units from multiple security branches engaging attackers at the same site.

Spatial analysis of the combat participation dataset (Dataset 1) provides strong evidence of widespread cross-organizational engagement. Fighting was documented across 32 geographically dispersed locations within the Gaza Envelope region, encompassing military bases, civilian communities, and transportation routes. These 32 locations recorded a total of 242 unit-location observations involving 145 distinct organizational units, with an average of 7.6 distinct units per location (SD = 6.5, median = 5.5, range 2–35).

The degree of organizational diversity is substantial. At 18 of the 32 responding locations (56.2%), personnel from two or more distinct organizational branches — IDF, Israel Police/Border Police, Shin Bet, or local defense teams — engaged attackers simultaneously. Four locations exhibited participation from three or more branches, and one location (Kibbutz Kfar Aza) recorded all four organizational categories operating together, with 35 distinct units engaging Hamas forces. The five locations with the highest unit diversity were Kfar Aza (35 units, 4 branches), Be'eri (21 units, 2 branches), Nahal Oz military base (14 units, 2 branches), Alumim (13 units, 1 branch), and Re'im (13 units, 3 branches). Unit diversity and branch diversity are positively correlated across locations (r = .59, p < .001), indicating that locations attracting more units also tended to draw from a wider range of organizational branches.

Special operations forces participated in the fighting at 24 of the 32 responding locations (75.0%), with an average of 2.5 SOF-affiliated units per location where they were present. The ubiquity of SOF participation across the battlespace — including at civilian communities far from their assigned operational sectors — provides further evidence that the response was driven by decentralized initiative rather than centralized deployment.

These patterns indicate that the defensive effort at most combat locations relied on cross-organizational cooperation rather than strictly hierarchical coordination. Personnel from different branches and units — many of whom had not previously operated together in a formal command structure — converged on the same locations and engaged the attacking force in what can be characterized as ad hoc team formation. This finding is consistent with **H4**, the Ad Hoc Team Formation Hypothesis, and with the mechanism proposed by the ERC framework: that shared tactical training and compatible operational procedures allowed individuals from different organizations to coordinate effectively in the absence of centralized planning.

### Organizational Diversity and Operational Stabilization

The fifth hypothesis proposed that locations characterized by decentralized participation and cross-organizational cooperation would experience more effective defensive outcomes. Testing this hypothesis requires careful attention to the direction of causality: locations that suffered the most severe attacks would be expected to attract both the largest and most diverse responses, creating a positive correlation between diversity and the intensity of the assault rather than between diversity and defensive effectiveness.

The analysis therefore adopts two complementary strategies. First, it compares locations that received an organized military response to those that did not, examining whether the presence of responding forces — regardless of their diversity — is associated with different outcomes. Second, within responding locations, it examines the relationship between organizational diversity and the composition of casualties, using kidnapping rates as a proxy for defensive failure (since kidnappings reflect attackers' ability to maintain prolonged control over an area).

**Response versus no response.** Five of the 37 documented locations received no organized military response during the October 7 attacks: Kibbutz Nir Oz, the Psyduck festival, Kibbutz Erez, Kibbutz Magen, and Moshav Ein HaBesor. The case of Nir Oz is particularly instructive. As the only civilian community that received no military reinforcement, Nir Oz suffered 76 kidnappings — more than half of all persons affected at that location (59.8% kidnapping ratio) and accounting for fully one-third of all kidnappings recorded across the 37 locations. By comparison, civilian communities that received an organized response — even those that suffered heavy casualties, such as Be'eri (30 kidnapped, 18.5% ratio, 21 responding units) and Kfar Aza (19 kidnapped, 17.0% ratio, 35 responding units) — experienced substantially lower kidnapping ratios. The starkest contrast is between Nir Oz (0 responding units, 59.8% kidnapping ratio) and adjacent kibbutzim that received organized responses: Alumim (13 units, 7.5%), Kisufim (11 units, 3.6%), and Re'im (13 units, 18.5%).

**Within responding locations.** Among the 25 civilian locations that received organized responses and for which outcome data are available, the relationship between organizational diversity and operational outcomes is complex. Locations with higher unit diversity tended to experience longer times to operational control (Pearson r = .79, p < .001) and more kidnappings (r = .50, p = .011). However, this positive correlation reflects the endogeneity described above: the most severely attacked communities — Be'eri, Kfar Aza, the Nova festival — attracted the largest and most diverse responses precisely because they were the primary targets. The correlation captures the severity of the attack, not the ineffectiveness of the response.

The more informative comparison is between the response and no-response conditions. Across the dataset, the evidence consistently shows that locations receiving any form of organized military response — whether composed of a few units or many — experienced outcomes that, while devastating, were characterized by the eventual halt of the attack and the prevention of mass kidnapping at the scale observed at Nir Oz. The rapid convergence of diverse forces on besieged locations created multiple points of resistance that complicated the attackers' ability to maintain control, consistent with the distributed response mechanism described by the ERC framework.

These findings provide partial support for **H5**, the Operational Stabilization Hypothesis, while highlighting important caveats. The most robust finding is the contrast between locations with and without organized responses: the absence of responding forces at Nir Oz was associated with catastrophically worse outcomes than at any community that received military reinforcement. Within responding locations, the relationship between organizational diversity and effectiveness is confounded by the severity of the attack, precluding a clean causal estimate. Future research with more granular temporal data — documenting the sequence in which specific units arrived and the corresponding changes in the operational situation — would allow for a stronger test of this hypothesis.
