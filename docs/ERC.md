On Saturday, October 7th, 2023, at 6:29 AM, approximately 6,000 Hamas operatives crossed the “smart barrier” (also known as the iron wall) - a set of fences and advanced sensors above and below ground - at 119 points along the Israel-Gaza border. Shortly after invading, forces from Hamas’s elite unit, the Nukhba, arrived at Re’im Base, Gaza Division’s Headquarters, and cut off its communication infrastructure (Meniv, 2024).[^fn1] Military organizations that lose their “core nerve system”, meaning the ability to receive situation reports, process data, and give commands, enter a systemic paralysis. Without direction, the units on the ground would not function until the chain of command is restored (Spek & Anderson, 2022).[^fn2] 
On October 7th, 2023, this was not the case. Thousands of combat soldiers - conscripts, reserves, police, and Shin Bet (Israel’s internal security agency)- spontaneously assembled at the Gaza border, where the attack transpired. They did so without receiving any orders, without proper communication systems, and while many of them were unarmed. Despite the difficult opening conditions, they managed to halt the attack, regain control over most of the civilian communities, and prevent the enemy from deepening its penetration into Israel within several hours. 
This paper asks, then, what conditions allowed for an effective military response despite the systematic collapse? We offer the framework of Embedded Response Capacity (ERC) as a basis for answering this research question and put it to an empirical test. ERC encompasses the characteristics that enable a force to respond effectively in a crisis in which communication systems and command-and-control structures are disabled, relying only on the skills of the responding forces. In positioning ERC within established theoretical frameworks, we draw on three complementary bodies of literature. 
High-reliability organizing (HRO) theory conceptualizes resilience as a set of organizing principles—preoccupation with failure, reluctance to simplify, sensitivity to operations, commitment to resilience, and deference to expertise—that collectively enable organizations to perform reliably under extreme uncertainty.[^fn3] ERC can be understood as a field-level, small-unit instantiation of these principles under conditions of C2 collapse. Latent organizing theory (Van Fenema & Romme, 2020) provides a second theoretical anchor, describing how pre-existing teams and training structures activate rapidly to address crises even without top-down coordination.[^fn4] Finally, systematic reviews of resilient performance in defense and security contexts confirm that individual-level capacities—including mental toughness, adaptive functioning, and shared tactical knowledge—underpin organizational resilience in precisely the conditions examined here (Jones et al., 2020).[^fn5]

This work contributes to understanding how armed forces and law enforcement agencies, confronted with new security challenges that exploit their difficulty in adapting, can still maintain enough flexibility to overcome these threats. This work can also help broaden understanding of organizational resilience beyond logistical preparedness, business crisis management, and responses to civilian crises, topics that most of the current literature addresses. Instead, it offers insights into organizational resilience as it manifests in the structural, cultural, and operational characteristics of fighting forces - an understanding that is becoming more and more relevant as many countries around the world find themselves responding to terrorism broadly, and swarm terrorism in particular. A methodological contribution this paper makes is the use of casualty analysis as a proxy for mapping tactical initiative and force mobilization in uncertain environments. This methodology is especially meaningful, as terrorism often creates situations in which it is difficult to trace all responding forces, as some are unaccounted for due to the nature of battles involving spontaneously joined forces. 
From a policy point of view, the paper offers a path towards new deployment models that are more in line with modern warfare. Instead of being regarded as a financial liability, the Israeli model of “People’s Army”, incorporating conscripts and reserves from most parts of society, the reserves being numerically more significant, can be reimagined as a critical operational asset in the reality of swarm warfare and hybrid terrorism, requiring fast deployment of forces that are not needed routinely.

Then, the concept of organizational resilience is explored to offer explanations for the success in countering swarm terrorism. The literature review is followed by the hypotheses and a methodological section that presents the extensive database created for this study and the stages of testing the hypotheses. Lastly, the findings are presented, followed by a discussion and concluding remarks.

Literature Review and Theoretical Framework


Swarm Terrorism
Swarming is a form of warfare with deep historical roots. Documented as early as the campaigns of the Scythians and Parthians in antiquity, and later refined by the Mongols into a sophisticated combined-arms doctrine, its defining logic has remained constant across millennia: dispersion, convergence, and the systematic overwhelming of a defender's capacity to respond coherently.[^fn6] In contemporary usage, swarming in the context of political violence refers to a pre-planned, coordinated, multi-vector attack in which separate cells strike simultaneously — or within a compressed temporal window — at multiple targets or at a single target from several directions, with the aim of maximizing casualties and material destruction.[^fn7]
Several properties follow from this definition. Swarming attacks are, by design, acentric: unlike linear engagements, they present no identifiable front and no single locus against which a defender can concentrate force. The division of attackers into autonomous cells threatens to overwhelm defenders by fragmenting their attention and response capacity simultaneously across multiple sites. This logic serves two related purposes. Tactically, it degrades the defender's ability to contain the attack; strategically, it amplifies the psychological effect — the hallmark, as Arquilla and Ronfeldt argue, of those actors seeking not merely to harm but to rupture societal cohesion.[^fn8]
The literature identifies two principal tactical variants. In a dispersed swarm, cells never coalesce into a single formation; they operate independently, coordinated in timing but separated in space. In a mass swarm, the attackers launch as a unified body and subsequently fracture into smaller groupings to strike distinct objectives. Both variants share a common operational logic: locate the target, converge and strike, then disperse — enabling successive waves or simply denying the defender a stable point of counterattack.[^fn9] For swarming to succeed, attackers must sustain elusiveness across each of these phases. This requires a combination of high mobility, concealment, superior situational awareness, protected communications, and the capacity to disrupt the adversary's own command and control infrastructure.[^fn10]
Swarming also has structural vulnerabilities. Its momentum depends on continuous movement; fixing cells in place neutralizes their primary advantage. Modifying the physical environment to reduce cover, severing logistical chains, and disrupting electronic communications can similarly degrade swarm effectiveness. Passive defenses — fortified perimeters, hardened communication nodes — may reduce the attacker's ability to exploit the gaps between defensive positions.[^fn11]

The trajectory of political violence over the past two decades reflects a marked and growing adoption of swarming tactics by violent non-state actors (VNSAs). Three cases illustrate this trend with particular clarity. In Mumbai on 26 November 2008, ten Lashkar-e-Taiba operatives — organized into five two-man cells — struck the Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Terminus railway station, the Leopold Café, the Taj Mahal Palace Hotel, the Oberoi Trident Hotel, and Nariman House within approximately forty minutes, killing 166 people. The attackers operated with GPS receivers, satellite phones, and VoIP communications that provided their Pakistani handlers with real-time situational awareness and the capacity for remote tactical direction — a technology-enabled command architecture that constituted a qualitative leap from prior terrorist operations.[^fn12]
In Paris on 13 November 2015, an ISIS cell of nine operatives, organized into three sub-teams, struck the Bataclan concert hall, a cluster of bars and restaurants in the 10th and 11th arrondissements, and the Stade de France within thirty minutes, killing 130 people. Months of advance logistical preparation — safe houses, rental vehicles, weapons caches — provided the operational infrastructure necessary for simultaneous strikes across the French capital.[^fn13]
Both attacks conform to a template of complex coordinated terrorist attacks (CCTAs) that scholars have identified as a distinct and evolving category of political violence. Across this class of incidents, a cluster of properties recurs: mobile assault teams operating with significant tactical autonomy; multi-site synchronization that fragments responder attention; extended durations that outlast initial police response cycles; and the deliberate targeting of communications and command infrastructure to paralyze the defender's ability to mount a coherent reaction.[^fn14]
The Hamas assault on southern Israel on 7 October 2023 both conforms to this template and extends it in ways that demand analytical attention.[^fn15] The scale, technological integration, and deliberate destruction of Israeli command and control systems represent a qualitative escalation beyond prior CCTAs — raising foundational questions about the adequacy of existing doctrines for countering swarm-style attacks. A central implication concerns the limits of top-down command in fast-moving, multi-node incidents: as Sullivan and Elkus observed in the wake of Mumbai, the situation on the ground in a swarm attack develops faster than senior echelons can process information and issue orders.[^fn16] In such conditions, the response capacity of those security personnel present at the scene of the attack becomes the decisive variable — a conclusion that has only grown more urgent in the light of October 7.

Crisis Governance, Organizational Resilience, and Embedded Response Capacity

Much of the literature on crisis governance assumes that effective response depends on the functioning of centralized coordination systems; this article argues that under conditions of systemic disruption, crisis performance may instead hinge on capacities embedded within frontline organizations themselves.

Despite extensive research on crisis governance and organizational resilience, existing frameworks still struggle to fully explain situations in which effective operational response emerges after the institutional architecture of crisis management has already failed. Most theories assume that coordination systems—communication networks, command structures, and interagency protocols—remain sufficiently functional to guide frontline action. Yet recent high-intensity crises reveal a different pattern: effective responses sometimes emerge precisely when those coordinating systems collapse. In such environments, the decisive variable may not be institutional preparedness but the extent to which frontline organizations possess embedded capacities enabling them to mobilize, coordinate, and act autonomously under extreme disruption.

To account for this pattern, the analysis shifts attention from the institutional architecture of crisis governance to the capabilities embedded within frontline organizations themselves. We conceptualize these capabilities as Embedded Response Capacity (ERC): the set of structural, cultural, and operational features that enable operational units to mobilize, coordinate, and act effectively when formal command-and-control systems are degraded or disabled.

Public administration scholarship increasingly treats crises as recurring tests of state capacity rather than exceptional disruptions. Contemporary crises—including terrorism, pandemics, cyberattacks, and natural disasters—often cross institutional and sectoral boundaries, producing what scholars describe as transboundary crises (Boin, 2019; Boin & Lodge, 2016). These events strain conventional governance arrangements because disruptions cascade across multiple systems simultaneously. In response, research has focused heavily on the institutional design of crisis-management systems capable of coordinating complex responses under uncertainty (Ansell et al., 2021; Comfort et al., 2010).

Within this perspective, crisis performance is largely understood as a function of institutional capacity—particularly governments’ ability to coordinate agencies, manage information flows, and centralize decision-making during emergencies (Boin et al., 2016). Studies of crisis governance therefore emphasize the design of emergency-management architectures such as incident command systems, crisis cabinets, and intergovernmental coordination networks. Organizational resilience is typically defined as the capacity of public institutions to absorb shocks and maintain core functions through robust governance structures (Boin & van Eeten, 2013; Duit, 2015).

While this literature has substantially advanced understanding of crisis preparedness and coordination, it also reflects an implicit assumption: that effective crisis response depends primarily on the functioning of centralized coordination mechanisms. Yet many crises unfold under conditions in which communication networks fail, situational awareness is incomplete, or command hierarchies are temporarily disrupted. Under such circumstances, the effectiveness of response may depend less on institutional design than on capabilities embedded within operational units themselves.

Existing research only partially addresses this problem. Studies of network governance demonstrate how preexisting relationships among organizations facilitate coordination during crises (Kapucu et al., 2022; Moynihan, 2009). Similarly, research on street-level bureaucracy highlights the discretionary role of frontline actors responding to rapidly evolving circumstances (Gofen & Lotta, 2021). These perspectives show that crisis response often depends on actors operating outside formal hierarchical control.

However, both literatures focus primarily on either interorganizational relationships or individual-level behavior. They devote less attention to the collective organizing capacities of operational units—groups that must mobilize and coordinate when the institutional systems designed to guide them temporarily fail. As a result, the literature lacks a systematic framework explaining how frontline organizations sustain coordinated action when the architecture of crisis governance itself becomes disrupted.

The literature on organizational resilience provides an important starting point for addressing this gap. Resilience is typically defined as the capacity of organizations to maintain or restore core functions in the face of disruption (Boin & van Eeten, 2013). Increasingly, scholars conceptualize resilience not as a static attribute but as an emergent property of complex organizational systems arising from the interaction of structural features and adaptive capabilities. Structural features include redundancy, reserve capacity, and contingency planning, while adaptive capabilities include improvisation, distributed problem-solving, and organizational learning.

Organizational theory provides further insight into how such adaptive capacities develop. One influential perspective emphasizes the role of sensemaking, through which actors interpret ambiguous events and align their actions under uncertainty (Weick, 1993; Maitlis & Christianson, 2014). Organizations that share common interpretive frameworks and operational repertoires are better able to reconstruct coordination when existing structures collapse.

Two additional strands of research illuminate how such embedded capacities emerge. The first concerns latent organizing. Latent organizing refers to institutional arrangements in which resources and coordination structures remain dormant during routine operations but can be rapidly activated during emergencies (Van Fenema & Romme, 2020). Examples include reserve systems and emergency task groups whose members hold ordinary roles during normal operations but mobilize collectively when crises occur. These arrangements allow organizations to generate mobilization capacity even when formal command structures are overwhelmed.

The second strand concerns high-reliability organizations (HROs). Research on HROs examines how organizations operating in hazardous environments maintain reliable performance despite high levels of risk and uncertainty (Weick et al., 1999). These organizations rely on distinctive organizing principles—including sensitivity to operations and deference to expertise—that allow authority to migrate toward actors with the most relevant operational knowledge during crises (Bigley & Roberts, 2001). Such principles facilitate rapid adaptation without requiring hierarchical instructions.

A third body of research focuses on organizational improvisation. Studies of bricolage and improvisation demonstrate how actors confronted with unexpected disruptions recombine available resources to produce workable solutions when established procedures prove inadequate (Baker & Nelson, 2005; Cunha et al., 1999). Improvisational responses often rely on shared professional repertoires—common training, doctrine, and language—that enable actors to coordinate even when they have not previously worked together.

Taken together, these literatures identify several mechanisms that support crisis response: latent organizing enables rapid mobilization, high-reliability organizing supports decentralized authority, and improvisational capabilities allow actors to adapt when established procedures fail. Yet these mechanisms are typically studied in isolation. None fully explains how organizations sustain coordinated action when multiple layers of institutional coordination—communication systems, command hierarchies, and coordination mechanisms—simultaneously break down.

Embedded Response Capacity

To address this analytical gap, this article introduces the concept of Embedded Response Capacity (ERC). ERC refers to the extent to which organizations possess embedded structural, cultural, and operational capabilities enabling frontline units to mobilize, coordinate, and act effectively when formal command-and-control systems are degraded or disabled.

ERC integrates insights from the literatures discussed above. First, latent organizing structures provide the mobilization infrastructure that enables rapid activation of personnel and resources. Second, high-reliability organizing principles allow authority to shift toward actors with relevant operational expertise when hierarchical coordination falters. Third, shared operational repertoires—including doctrine, training, and professional language—enable improvised teams to coordinate effectively even when they have not previously served together in the same operational unit.

Embedded Response Capacity emerges from the interaction of these three organizational capabilities—latent organizing structures, high-reliability organizing principles, and shared operational repertoires. Because these elements reinforce one another, the absence or weakness of any single component significantly constrains the ability of frontline actors to mobilize, coordinate, and exercise initiative under conditions of systemic disruption.

By synthesizing these mechanisms, ERC shifts analytical attention from the institutional architecture of crisis governance to the embedded capacities of frontline organizations. This perspective highlights how decentralized operational capabilities can generate coordinated action even when centralized command systems temporarily fail.

Propositions

The ERC framework generates several observable implications regarding crisis response under conditions of degraded command-and-control systems.

First, organizations possessing stronger latent organizing structures—such as reserve systems and preconfigured emergency units—should exhibit faster mobilization and broader participation in crisis response once disruption becomes evident.

Second, organizations whose professional cultures incorporate high-reliability organizing principles should display greater tactical initiative among lower-level actors when hierarchical coordination breaks down.

Third, organizations characterized by strong shared operational repertoires should demonstrate more effective ad hoc coordination among actors who have not previously served together within the same operational unit.

Taken together, these propositions imply that effective crisis response under systemic disruption depends less on centralized command systems than on the extent to which organizations possess embedded capacities enabling decentralized mobilization, sensemaking, and coordination.

Empirical Strategy
Testing the ERC framework presents an empirical challenge. In crises characterized by communication breakdowns and fragmented command structures, conventional operational records often fail to capture the full scope of responding forces. Units and individuals frequently mobilize without formal orders and operate outside established reporting chains, leaving gaps in official documentation. To address this problem, this study adopts a casualty-based analytical strategy. Casualty data provide a uniquely informative proxy for operational engagement: when personnel are killed or wounded, they were necessarily present at the point of contact with the adversary. By mapping the distribution, timing, and organizational affiliations of casualties, it becomes possible to reconstruct patterns of mobilization and tactical initiative under conditions of systemic disruption. In this sense, casualty analysis offers a window into the decentralized operational dynamics that ERC is expected to generate. If organizations possess strong Embedded Response Capacity, casualty patterns should reveal rapid and widespread engagement by diverse units and individuals, including actors operating outside formal deployment frameworks.


Hypotheses 

Latent Organizing Hypothesis: The strength of latent organizing structures within military organizations—including reserve networks, pre-configured emergency units, and cross-unit training ties—will be positively associated with the speed and geographic coverage of force mobilization following a large-scale unexpected attack, net of formal command-and-control performance.
Deference to Expertise Hypothesis: Military units operating under high levels of deference to frontline expertise will exhibit greater operational adaptability—measured by timely local initiative, context-appropriate deviation from plan, and rapid resource reallocation—than units constrained by rigid rank-based authority structures, particularly under conditions of degraded command-and-control and high environmental uncertainty.
Shared Repertoires Hypothesis: Ad hoc military teams whose members share common training, doctrine, and tactical language will display higher coordination quality—reflected in reduced fratricide risk, fewer contradictory actions, and more coherent task allocation—than teams lacking such shared professional repertoires, all else equal.
ERC Interaction Hypothesis: The positive effect of latent organizing on crisis performance will be amplified when units simultaneously exhibit high levels of deference to frontline expertise and strong shared professional repertoires; in the absence of these cultural and cognitive enablers, latent organizing alone will yield weaker and more variable gains in operational resilience.


DATA AND METHODS
Research Design
Testing the five ERC hypotheses against the October 7 attack requires a research design that can trace causal mechanisms within a single critical case while generating systematic variation across units and locations. We adopt a mixed-methods approach that combines logistic regression analysis of a casualty-based dataset, structured cross-unit comparison using a locations database, and qualitative process tracing of selected tactical engagements. 
This combination is appropriate given the structure of the October 7 events: the attack unfolded across dozens of discrete sites involving distinct combinations of responding forces, producing natural variation in ERC endowments and performance outcomes that can be analyzed both statistically and comparatively. No single method is sufficient here. The logistic regression addresses the Latent Organizing and Deference to Expertise hypotheses at the individual and unit level; the structured comparison across locations addresses the Shared Repertoires and Interaction hypotheses; and process tracing of specific engagements speaks to the ERC Recovery hypothesis, which requires longitudinal evidence reaching beyond the acute two-day window.
The case of October 7 presents both advantages and limitations as a research site. Its principal advantage is analytic leverage: the attack constitutes a near-total collapse of the formal command-and-control architecture against which ERC mechanisms can be observed in relatively clean isolation from institutional coordination. Where responses succeeded, the success cannot be attributed to centralized planning or early warning. Its limitation is that the case is singular, which prevents cross-national inference. We address this constraint not by overstating generalizability but by treating the case as a theory-building exercise that illustrates the conditions under which ERC components operate and interact.
Data
The empirical analysis draws on three complementary databases constructed specifically for this study. Together they provide coverage of personnel characteristics, unit behavior, and locational outcomes across the two days of the attack (October 7–8, 2023).
The first is a fatalities database containing detailed records on 369 security personnel killed during the attack and its immediate aftermath. The dataset integrates four primary sources: the IDF's dedicated online register of fallen soldiers, which records each individual's name, age, rank, unit, and date of death; the Israel Police's memorial website, which provides equivalent information for 68 fallen officers; the Kan public broadcasting service's geo-referenced narrative database; and the civilian-initiated Mapping the Massacre project. Because no single official source is exhaustive—each agency published its own partial record—the dataset was constructed by cross-referencing all four sources and supplementing gaps with media reporting and family testimonies regarding time and location of death. The resulting dataset records 261 IDF personnel, 58 police officers, 7 Shin Bet operatives, and 43 members of civilian emergency response teams. Of the total, 149 were stationed in the Gaza Envelope at the time of the attack and 218 joined the fighting subsequently; 343 were killed in combat and 24 as non-combatants.
The fatalities database functions as a proxy for the broader population of forces that operated on the ground. Because an individual's presence at a given location and time is confirmed by the circumstances of their death, the dataset permits inference about which units were active at each site throughout the two-day period—inference that cannot be drawn from official deployment records alone, most of which remain classified. The proxy logic follows the established use of administrative outcome records to reconstruct organizational behavior when direct observational data is unavailable (Honig 2019). The key individual-level variables are: branch affiliation (IDF, Police, Shin Bet, Emergency Response Team); Special Operations Forces status (binary); officer rank (binary); gender; and behavioral outcome—specifically, whether the individual joined the fighting independently before receiving formal orders (coded 1) or responded only within their assigned station (coded 0).
The second database records the 37 distinct locations at which combat occurred during the attack, specifying for each site the responding units, the time the battle began, the number of fatalities, and the number of civilians and security personnel abducted. Of the 37 locations, 32 received some form of military response and 5 received none; 26 of the 32 that received a response were reached by Special Operations Forces. This database is the primary source for cross-unit comparison and enables analysis of the relationship between force composition and locational outcomes.
The third source is a set of qualitative materials—IDF and police after-action investigations released to the press, survivor and operator testimonies, and investigative journalism based on unit-level reconstruction of specific engagements—used to trace the mechanisms underlying statistical patterns identified in the quantitative analysis. These materials are used selectively to examine process rather than frequency: the aim is to understand how ERC components produced (or failed to produce) adaptive responses at specific sites, not to generate additional variation across cases.
Analytic Strategy
The dependent variable in the primary quantitative analysis is the binary joining outcome—whether an individual entered the fight independently before formal mobilization orders—which serves as the principal behavioral indicator of latent organizing and deference to expertise at the individual level. We estimate logistic regression models with robust standard errors, entering Special Operations Forces status, officer rank, branch affiliation, and the hour of the incident as independent variables. Descriptive statistics and inferential results are reported in the standard JPART format, with coefficient tables accompanied by predicted probability plots to facilitate substantive interpretation.
For the locations database, the analysis is structured as a most-similar systems comparison across the 37 combat sites. Sites are coded on three ERC dimensions—presence of SOF-trained personnel, evidence of cross-unit prior training ties (drawn from qualitative sources), and documented instances of independent local initiative—and performance outcomes are measured by the ratio of fatalities to personnel present and the time-to-site-clearance where that information is available. This comparison addresses the interaction hypothesis directly, since it allows assessment of whether sites with multiple ERC components outperform sites with only one.
Process tracing is applied to four purposively selected engagements—selected to maximize variation in ERC endowment and outcome—to identify the specific mechanisms linking ERC components to tactical performance.

HYPOTHESES AND DATA LINKAGE
The five hypotheses map onto the three databases as follows, with data gaps flagged where additional evidence would be required to achieve a complete test.
Latent Organizing Hypothesis. The fatalities database is the primary source. The joining variable operationalizes the behavioral expression of latent organizing: individuals who entered the fight before receiving orders did so through pre-existing social ties, neighborhood networks, or standing emergency protocols rather than through formal command activation. The hour-of-incident variable captures mobilization speed across the two-day window. The locations database supplements this by identifying which sites were reached first and by what types of forces. 
Data gap: the fatalities database cannot directly measure the density of pre-existing social network ties among responders, which is the theoretical construct latent organizing refers to. Testing this hypothesis fully requires supplementary network data—ideally from reserve unit rosters and documented WhatsApp or informal coordination chains—that is not currently in the dataset.
Deference to Expertise Hypothesis. The SOF status and officer rank variables in the fatalities database are the key predictors. If deference to frontline expertise characterizes SOF culture more than conventional unit culture, SOF-affiliated individuals should display higher joining rates net of rank and branch. The officer rank variable functions as a partial negative control: in units with rigid hierarchy, higher rank should suppress independent action; in units with high deference to expertise, it should not. 
Data gap: this hypothesis requires unit-level cultural data—specifically, documented training protocols and command philosophy—to move beyond structural proxies. After-action testimonies and the qualitative process-tracing material partially address this gap, but a systematic survey of unit doctrine would strengthen the inference considerably.
Shared Repertoires Hypothesis. The locations database is the primary source. Sites at which responding forces shared prior joint training or belonged to overlapping unit networks can be compared against sites assembled from heterogeneous personnel with no common operational history. The performance indicators are fratricide incidents (where documented), time to site clearance, and the ratio of attackers neutralized to total responding force. The qualitative material is essential here: shared repertoires are not directly observable in administrative records and must be reconstructed from testimony and unit histories. 
Data gap: systematic fratricide and friendly-fire data for October 7 is not publicly available and would need to be extracted from classified IDF investigative records or obtained through formal data-access requests.
ERC Interaction Hypothesis. This is the most data-demanding of the hypotheses. It requires that each location be coded on all three ERC dimensions simultaneously—latent organizing, deference to expertise, and shared repertoires—so that the interaction effects can be estimated. The locations database provides the outcome data; the fatalities database and qualitative materials provide the unit-level ERC codings. The structured comparison across the 32 sites that received a response generates sufficient variation to test whether sites with all three components present outperform sites with only one or two, but the small N limits statistical power. 
Data gap: this hypothesis would benefit substantially from declassified IDF unit readiness assessments from the eve of October 7, which would allow independent pre-attack coding of ERC levels rather than coding from the events themselves—a potential endogeneity problem that the current design does not fully resolve.



The locations database is further enriched by a systematic open-source reconstruction of unit-level deployment footprints across the 37 combat sites. Because no consolidated IDF movement log for the first 48 hours has been released publicly, this reconstruction treats explicitly named unit–location attributions drawn from official IDF press releases and published IDF battle probe narratives as a lower-bound measure of multi-site deployment. A location is counted as verified only when a named source directly attributes a specific unit's operation at a named site—kibbutz, military post, junction, or named road segment treated as a discrete engagement area in a probe narrative. Social media reports and unverified accounts are excluded entirely.
Within these constraints, the reconstruction identifies meaningful variation in multi-location deployment footprints across the units that responded during the first 48 hours. 
Shayetet 13 (IDF Naval Commando) and Yamam (Israel Police Counter-Terrorism Unit) achieved the broadest verified footprints, each operating at six distinct named locations. Shayetet 13's engagements are documented at Sufa military post, Kibbutz Be'eri, Kibbutz Kfar Aza, Kibbutz Sa'ad, Kibbutz Mefalsim, and Kibbutz Nir Oz, based on an IDF-attributed enumeration of the unit's ground engagements. Yamam's six locations—Moshav Yakhini, Ofakim, Shaar Hanegev Junction, Kibbutz Mefalsim, Kibbutz Re'im, and Kibbutz Be'eri—are each confirmed by separate published IDF probe narratives, several of which also document the unit's sequential movement between sites. 
Maglan (IDF Commando) follows with five verified locations, including engagements along Route 232 and the Black Arrow memorial site in addition to three kibbutzim. 
Sayeret Matkal (IDF General Staff Reconnaissance) is documented at four distinct locations. Shaldag (IAF Commando) and Shin Bet operational personnel each appear at three verified locations, while the Golani Brigade's 13th Battalion is confirmed at two. The remaining units in the reconstruction—including Duvdevan, Yahalom, Yamas, Unit 669, and several others—are each documented at a single named location.
One sequential movement is directly supported by the source material: Yamam forces that concluded clearing operations at Moshav Yakhini by midday redeployed to Ofakim, where fighting continued. 
A second movement—from the Mefalsim area toward Kibbutz Re'im—is indicated by probe narrative language but does not carry explicit unit-level attribution and should be read as a directional indicator rather than a confirmed route. 
For Shayetet 13, the IDF-attributed enumeration establishes multi-site presence but does not specify the chronological order of engagements across those locations.
Three structural limitations shape how this reconstruction can be used analytically. First, the absence of a consolidated official deployment record means the unit rankings reflect what has been probed and publicly documented, not necessarily the full operational picture. Units with substantial activity may be underrepresented simply because their engagements were not the subject of a published probe narrative. Second, attribution granularity varies: probe narratives frequently refer to "troops," "police," or "special forces" without naming constituent units, suppressing unit-level resolution in ways that are not random—smaller, more secretive units such as Shin Bet sub-formations are systematically undercounted relative to their actual presence. Third, location taxonomy is not fully consistent across sources, with some narratives emphasizing junction-level engagements and others kibbutz-level ones, creating potential inflation or deflation of footprint counts depending on the probe's frame of reference.
Despite these constraints, the reconstruction contributes directly to the empirical strategy. The multi-location footprint measure—units operating at more sites under fragmented command—functions as a behavioral indicator of both latent organizing capacity and operational adaptability. Units that reached more locations in less time, without centralized direction, exhibit the signature of ERC in action. The co-appearance of Yamam and Shin Bet operational personnel at three of the same locations, each confirmed independently by probe narratives, also provides preliminary evidence of shared-repertoire coordination operating across agency lines. Completing this reconstruction systematically—by ingesting all published probe summaries, estimated at approximately 40 battle-level investigations, and extracting every explicit unit–location statement—would materially strengthen the locations database and reduce the current lower-bound bias before the comparative analysis is finalized.

Context 
The Attack 
Hamas’s success relied on strategic deception. In the years leading up to the attack, Hamas made Israel believe it was deterred and was solely interested, at this point, in financial benefits in the shape of Qatari monetary assistance and permits for Gazans to work in Israel. Even during the “March of Return” protests, Hamas made sure to let Israel know it does not aim for further escalation of tensions (Nakhoul & Saul, 2023). 

This relative quiet allowed Hamas to plan and execute a simultaneous attack that took advantage of Israel’s static defense posture. On Saturday, October 7th, 2023, at 6:29 a.m., under a barrage of thousands of rockets, 3,800 militants from Hamas's elite “Nukhba” force launched Operation Al-Aqsa Flood. The militants breached the barrier that stood on the border between Israel and Gaza at 119 points using explosive devices and tractors that they had placed near the border in advance (Meniv, 2024). Most notably, in the weekly riots on the border, so-called “The Great March of Return”, Hamas terrorists assimilated in the crowd, using the disruption to plant explosives, while the IDF was preoccupied with the masses of people marching towards the fence.


After breaching the fence, they split into heavily armed squads equipped with AK-47 rifles, RPG rockets, hand grenades, incendiary devices, walkie-talkies, and maps. Their comrades, deployed along the border, provided the raiders with continuous assistance. They launched anti-tank missiles and drones carrying explosives at the observation and monitoring equipment of the Israeli border protection apparatus, neutralizing them. Organized in squads, many militants traveled in pickup trucks with heavy machine guns mounted on them. Others rode motorcycles or hovered using powered paragliders, the latter landing easily inside Israeli civilian communities. Every squad headed to its predesignated target. The targets included eight military bases and outposts, three urban centers, twenty-six agricultural settlements, and open areas where music festivals occurred. Hamas naval commandos set out in boats to another open target - Zikim Beach. Hamas militants took over military outposts and multiple civilian communities within a matter of hours. 


The Hamas offensive was a carefully planned raid. The first wave of attackers, members of Hamas’s elite unit, relied on surprise, capitalizing on their numerous advantages. These advantages included a significant numerical superiority, the availability of weapons and ammunition suitable for a raid, and detailed tactical blueprints. They stormed the Israeli army posts and bases near the border, attacked the communications infrastructure, and neutralized the military's command and control infrastructure. They also set up ambushes on the roads leading to the area under attack, isolating it and preventing reinforcements from arriving. (Dvori, 2023).

The attack, by using multiple techniques and targets, harmed Israel’s ability to respond by blinding it by targeting the cameras and sensor systems; by cutting off almost all communications between commanding echelons and ground forces by targeting communication systems; and by firing thousands of rockets towards Israel, forcing soldiers to enter safe rooms, instead of engaging the enemy at the border and around military bases and civilian communities. Hamas's numerical advantage was also significant. Only about 760 soldiers, some of them, like the observants, were unarmed, faced thousands of invaders carrying superior equipment and in larger numbers (Fabian, 2025). According to data collected in this work, IDF forces stationed at the Gaza border on the eve of the attack included the observants, combat soldiers from the 13th and 51st battalions of the Golani infantry brigade, and from the 77th battalion of the 7th armored brigade. Hence, only three combat battalions and no special operations units were stationed at the Gaza border at that time. It is important to note that October 7th, 2023, was also a Jewish holiday, and so many soldiers were given the weekend off. 
In numerical terms, the ratio between IDF soldiers and invaders was almost 1:8. In military terms, this ratio all but guarantees a swift defeat, especially when the attacker enjoys the advantage of a strategic surprise (Bar-Joseph, 2008). Indeed, the military bases on the frontline were quickly captured or encircled, and communications lines were cut off or burned, which allowed Hamas to isolate the area and prevent almost all communication between those on the frontlines and IDF, police, and Shin Bet headquarters. To further isolate them, Hamas’s Nukhba forces also ambushed those driving on the main roads leading to and from the Gaza Envelope.

ERC and the Israeli Security Apparatus  
Most Israeli special forces personnel conscripts and reservists alike are designed with a primary and a secondary operational role. The primary role is domain-specific: Tier 1 units including Sayeret Matkal,  Shaldag, and Shayetet 13 are optimized for tasks like deep reconnaissance, long-range raids, forward air control, or naval special warfare, often at strategic depth and usually in the context of interstate conflict.  In institutional terms, they belong to Israel’s warfighting toolbox: they are selected, trained, and equipped to give political and military leaders high-end options in conventional or high-intensity campaigns, from precision strikes in distant theaters to shaping operations ahead of major ground offensives. 
At the same time, these professionals, as well as all other SOF, inhabit a secondary role that is tied to the permanent, low-intensity struggle against terrorism that runs through Israel’s daily security environment. 
Police and Border Police units most notably Yamam and Yamas epitomize this, with a formal mandate for domestic counterterrorism and hostage rescue, but military SOF also move continuously between strategic tasks and “routine” counterterrorism missions in the West Bank, Gaza periphery, and sometimes beyond.  

This dual-purpose design—professional for war and special operations, routine for counterterrorism—meant that on October 7 Israel did not mobilize a set of “pure” warfighting units suddenly re-tasked to terrorism, but rather flipped a large, already hybrid ecosystem into a higher, more violent gear. The same SOF and elite police communities that, in quieter periods, spend most of their time on arrests, interdictions, targeted killings, and hostage contingencies were abruptly required to fight battalion-level engagements in Israeli border communities, conduct complex raids and rescues, and then transition into sustained operations in Gaza and other arenas.  The October 7 shock therefore revealed not only gaps in readiness and deployment, but also the underlying logic of the Israeli system: a dense web of special operations and police units whose personnel are socialized from the outset into holding a primary role in specialized warfighting domains and a secondary, but constant, role in counterterrorism, and whose rapid mobilization and adaptation are built into peacetime routines rather than improvised only at the onset of war. 

Understanding Organizational Resilience - Findings

In this sense, resilience encompasses not only the ability to anticipate and prevent crises but also the capacity to absorb disruption, adapt, and recover operational effectiveness.


The Israeli response on October 7 illustrates precisely this dimension of resilience. Despite the collapse of the Gaza Division’s command infrastructure and the absence of coordinated deployment orders, military personnel, reservists, police units, and civilians rapidly mobilized and engaged the attackers. Within hours, these decentralized forces halted further penetration into Israel and gradually reestablished control over most affected communities.

The October 7 attack fits this description closely. The assault targeted military installations, civilian communities, and infrastructure simultaneously, creating a multi-domain emergency that required immediate responses from military, police, intelligence, and civilian actors. Under such conditions, the rapid mobilization of forces without central coordination is difficult to explain using conventional crisis-management models alone.

Latent Organizing - Findings

In the Israeli case, latent organizing is visible in the rapid mobilization of infantry soldiers and officers, including reservists, as well as police officers who converged on combat zones shortly after the attack began. These individuals underwent similar training in had and shared a common operational language which enabled them to coordinate actions without formal orders.

High-reliability organization (HRO) - Findings
The dynamics observed on October 7 reflect this principle. With communication between headquarters and field units disrupted, tactical initiative shifted to platoon commanders, special-operations personnel, experienced reservists, and police officers already present in the affected areas. These actors interpreted the evolving situation and initiated local responses without awaiting formal authorization. In this sense, the Israeli response illustrates how HRO principles can emerge organically when hierarchical command structures collapse.

Shared Capabilities
The Israeli military structure, based on universal conscription and a large reserve force, produces precisely such shared professional knowledge. Infantry soldiers, reservists, and many members of security agencies receive similar counterterrorism training, creating a common doctrinal foundation that facilitates ad hoc cooperation.

Flexibility and improvisation 
The early hours of the October 7 response displayed many of these features. Informal groups of soldiers, reservists, police officers, and civilians coordinated through personal networks and mobile communication technologies, creating temporary operational structures that later integrated into a more organized military response.

Bricolage
Evidence from the October 7 response suggests that such improvisation was widespread: civilian vehicles served as transport and command platforms, digital messaging platforms replaced disrupted military communications, and improvised weapons were used in situations where formal equipment was unavailable.

The Database 
The fog of war on October 7th, 2023, prevented the creation and maintenance of a unified log of all those who participated in the fighting. Moreover, in other cases, such information was classified by security forces, preventing researchers from accessing it. Therefore, this work utilizes data on security forces fatalities (n=369) who were killed on October 7th and 8th, 2023, as a proxy. The data was collected from official open sources and was cross-verified:
1. The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) records. The IDF’s dedicated mini-site details each soldier’s name in Hebrew and English, age, rank, unit, and date of falling. In total, we recorded 301 cases from this source. Since Hebrew is a gendered language, the soldier’s gender can also be inferred from this source (Israel Defense Forces, 2023). 
2. Police records. The October 7th, 2023, attack’s fallen police officers were added to the Israeli Police’s general website dedicated to all police personnel who died while serving in the force. Each policeman’s dedicated page includes their name, age, rank, unit, date of falling, place of burial, and details about their family. We recorded 68 cases from this source (Israel Police, 2023). 
3. Israel’s Public Broadcasting Service’s database. Kan, Israel’s public broadcasting service, created an online map, telling the stories of communities and individuals affected by the Hamas attack. The map is not exhaustive as it accounts for only some of the events that transpired in each location. Moreover, it is primarily focused on civilians affected, and fallen security personnel are mostly mentioned only in relation to the civilian victims. The dataset is narrative, so the information available for victims is inconsistent, and some victims may have more detailed pages than others (Kan, 2023). 
4. Civilian-created databases. Several independent initiatives emerged in the aftermath of the October 7th, 2023, attack to document that day's events. The main civilian initiative, Mapping the Massacre, also takes the form of a map, like Kan's dataset. The focus of this initiative is to map the exact location of each of the victims of the attack, including those abducted and injured. In this dataset as well, there are inconsistencies in the information provided for each victim. They are, however, classified based on their affiliation as civilians, security personnel, etc. (Mapping the Massacres 2023). 


The sources mentioned above do not provide all the necessary information for a thorough analysis. For example, the exact location and time of death of all security personnel who perished during the Attack can not be accounted for relying solely on these sources. Therefore, information was also collected and verified using news articles published and testimonies of the nuclear families of the perished, who indicated when the contact with their loved ones was cut off, or provided information they had received from the military, police, or Shin Bet, depending on where their loved one served. Additionally, operational investigations conducted by security agencies were only made public by the press, so this research relied on media outlets for this information.

This approach — using security forces fatalities as a proxy for the responding force — offers a significant methodological advantage in a context where comprehensive participation records are either classified or nonexistent. By construction, the dataset captures only those whose presence on the battlefield is independently verifiable through multiple, cross-validated official sources. While a complete roster of all responders cannot be reconstructed, the fatality data provides a systematic record of confirmed combatants whose participation is beyond dispute. We further acknowledge that this method introduces the potential for survivor bias: individuals who fought and survived may carry characteristics that differ from those who fought and perished. Critically, however, the likely direction of this bias is one that makes our estimates conservative rather than inflated. If officers and special operations personnel are systematically more likely to survive a given engagement owing to superior tactical skill — a reasonable assumption — then their representation in the fatality data constitutes a lower bound of their actual participation. In other words, the proportions of officers and SOF personnel reported in the self-joined group likely underestimate their true prevalence among voluntary responders. This conservative interpretation is reinforced by the consistency of the directionality across multiple independent variables (branch, service type, officer status, and SOF status), all of which point in the same direction, making a systematic reverse-bias unlikely. Future research employing survivor interviews, unit-level after-action reviews, or declassified operational logs would provide a more complete picture of the responding force’s composition.

The pattern of conservative bias documented here is consistent with findings from CCTA reconstruction studies and mass-casualty research more broadly, where fatality-based datasets systematically underrepresent the most skilled and experienced responders owing to their higher survival probability (Sullivan & Elkus, 2009). The multi-source triangulation methodology employed in this study—cross-referencing IDF official records, Police records, the Kan public broadcasting database, and civilian mapping initiatives—follows recognized best practice in retrospective CCTA reconstruction, where classified operational logs are unavailable and verified open-source records constitute the primary evidentiary base. While no single source is exhaustive, the cross-validation protocol ensures that each case in the database is corroborated by at least two independent sources, minimizing inclusion error and supporting the internal validity of the proxy measure.


For each of the fallen, the following information was recorded: 
1. Personal data: name and age.  
1. Organizational affiliation: military, police, Shin Bet, emergency squad. 
1. Unit: specific identification such as Golani 13th battalion, or the Sderot station of the Israel police. 
1. Service status: conscript, professional, or reserve. 
1. Service characteristics: binary variables to describe combat/non-combat, officer/non-officer, special operations unit/not, counterterrorism unit/not, and a non-binary variable to describe the role and rank. 
1. Place of death: name of community, military base, road, etc. (including coordinates). 
1. Time of death: the time relative to the beginning of the attack. For example, 9 AM is recorded as the 3rd hour of the attack. 
A note on the time-of-death variable as an effectiveness measure: comparing elapsed time between the joined and the not-joined groups directly is subject to an arrival-time confound, since voluntary responders spent additional time traveling to the Gaza Envelope before entering combat. To partially control for this, the ANOVA analysis compares elapsed time across service-status categories (conscripts, professionals, reserves) within the first wave — i.e., among individuals who were already present at the onset of the attack. The finding that professionals outlasted conscripts within this group, where both had the same exposure from the beginning, provides a more reliable signal of an effectiveness differential that is not driven by arrival timing. This dataset serves as a proxy for information regarding the units and agencies that operated on the ground in Southern Israel on October 7-8, 2023. By accounting for the affiliations of each fallen security personnel, one can infer which units and individual combatants took part in the fighting. Furthermore, by combining this data with the time and place of death for each fallen security personnel, one can determine which unit operated in each location at any given time during these two days. Description of the data  
In the database, N=369. Out of all those security personnel examined, 25 were killed as non-combatants, in their homes or as attendees in the Nova Festival, and did not join the fighting once it erupted. Of the 344 killed in combat, 145 joined the fighting, while 198 were on active duty on the morning of October 7th, 2023; data were missing for 1 individual. Hence, for N=344 killed in combat, 42.15% joined, while 57.56% were on active duty. This distinction is operationally important as those who were stationed in the Gaza Envelope at the time of the attack were the first ones to respond, and ultimately fall. Those who joined, on the other hand, comprise the “second wave” of responders. 
Moreover, indeed, for those stationed at the Gaza Envelope, the mean time between the start of the attack and their falling is 4 hours, compared to 6.2 hours for those who joined. These two hours represent the time it took for those who were not stationed elsewhere or on active duty to join the fighting. The average time from the beginning of the attack until death for the entire population was 5 hours, with a significant standard deviation of 5.1. This data supports H0, arguing that there were two distinct waves of respondents to the Hamas attack.

Anova analysis
Anova analysis reinforces these findings. The two meaningful differences in time elapsed between the start of the attack and the time of death are for branches and for service status. Overall, those belonging to the IDF lasted the most hours, followed by the police, emergency response teams, and the Shin Bet. This could be explained by the fact that the vast majority of respondents in both waves (over 70%) are military personnel. The difference in service status, however, is indicative: the reserves lasted longest, followed by professionals, and lastly conscripts. While the data on reserves can be explained by the fact that they joined mostly in the second wave, the data on professionals versus conscripts means that the joiners, a group more abundant with officers, lasted longer and were more effective than the first wave of non-joiners, composed mostly of conscripts. Thus, while approximately 57.5% of responders were at the scene when Hamas attacked, the data support H1, that the most effective combat forces joined voluntarily. Without these forces, rich with officers and SoFs, the penetration into Israeli territory is speculated to have been deeper and lasted longer. 


On October 7th, 2023, the fighting spread along the entire Gaza Envelope, from the Zikim Beach in the north to Kerem Shalom in the south, and from Netiv HaAsara, only a few hundred meters from the fence, to Ofakin, 22 kilometers from the border. In this database, we grouped locations so roads are analyzed as the same location unit as the communities they lead to, etc. Moreover, here as well, there are observable differences between joined and not joined. The former fought in 31 locations, while the latter fought in 28. Furthermore, those who joined perished mostly in civilian locations (80.65%) as opposed to in military bases (19.35%). Those who did not join exhibited more mixed results (60.7% of locations are civilian, while 39.29% are military bases). This means that Israel managed to field a “defensive swarm” in the face of Hamas’s swarm attack, engaging the invaders in multiple locations, most notably in the second wave of responders. In almost every location, with the exception of places like Nir Oz, the invaders were met with resistance, whether by an emergency response team, a local policeman, or an off-duty IDF officer. This constant friction slowed down the invaders and prevented some murders and abductions. 


 The central characteristics of Embedded Response Capacity highlighted in this paper are joint training, decentralized execution and decision-making, urban warfare, adaptability, and ad hoc teaming. 
1. Most of the self-joined forces (72%) belong to the IDF and to emergency response teams. Both these groups undergo the same training in the same IDF facilities and use a similar operational language, thereby satisfying the joint training requirement. 
2. The responders operated in small, isolated teams, while the Gaza Division was practically defunct, and the IDF Headquarters was unable to communicate with those on the ground. Thus, both the execution and decision-making processes were decentralized. 
3. All combat soldiers in the IDF, the Israel Police, and the Shin Bet undergo urban warfare training. The fact that 88% of those who joined are combat soldiers indicates that they are all familiar with urban warfare. 
4. While the first wave of responders, those stationed in the Gaza Envelope, were unable to adapt and were killed in their bases, the second wave was able to adapt better and engage the attackers in multiple locations - on the roads, inside civilian communities, etc. 
5. Those who joined did so without being called upon and without their organic combat team. Instead, they formed ad hoc teams organized by location and skills to deliver the most effective response possible at the time. 
Therefore, the data also supports H2, that the basic characteristics of ERC  were reflected in self-joined forces.

Discussion and Conclusions 
How can such a disorganized and spontaneous response be so effective? This was the main question this paper explored. The answer, as we demonstrated, lies with embedded response capacity. In an organized, hierarchical system, information flows from the bottom up, while commands flow from top down. However, on October 7th, 2023, like in other terrorist swarming incidents targeting communications, the ground forces and the commanders were unable to communicate. Thus, information remained at the ground level, never making its way up, and commands never reached the fighting forces from the commanders. 


Without ERC, a military, or any other system in crisis for that matter, unable to communicate, will be unlikely to manage and respond to the crisis adequately. However, ERC mitigates the lack of commands from higher echelons and leverages ingenuity, initiative, and preparedness. Therefore, the fighting forces, who possess the most accurate information and situational awareness, are able to grasp the threats they face and craft a suitable response without relying on the headquarters for direction and guidance. On October 7th, 2023, thousands of tactical decisions were made in real time, on the ground, replacing the generals’ wisdom with that of the trained and resilient forces. Those decisions not only minimized civilian harm but also coalesced into a wider response that allowed Israel to retake the Gaza Envelope from Hamas within several days. A colossal intelligence failure led the Israeli security establishment to miss all indicators. However, unlike the intelligence and the IDF command, the response on the ground, by ad hoc teams of soldiers, policemen, emergency response team members, and Shin Bet operatives, allowed Israel to turn the situation on its head and regain control relatively quickly.

This research identified a recurring pattern of squads composed of individuals who had never trained together, in many cases belonging to different units and serving at different times. What tied these squads together was that they were all present at the same place, at the same time—two main elements allowed this ad-hoc teaming to work: standardization and trust. As previously mentioned, all past and present IDF soldiers are familiar with the same tactical commands, signals, and combat tactics, which enable ad hoc teams to work together effectively without dedicating time to coordination and planning. On top of that, the existing basic trust within the serving communities in Israeli society, contrasted with the brutality of the October 7th, 2023, attack, unifies these formerly foreign individuals and turns them into an effective combat squad. 

Comparing the Israeli response to the Hamas attack to the Indian response to the Mumbai attack highlights key differences that made the Israeli response far more successful than the Indian response. The failure of the Indian forces to respond effectively to a much smaller-scale attack was centered on the forces’ lack of operational police art, defined as the ability to manage more than one incident in one location and to cope with their consequences at a given time. Operational police art is essential in responding to swarm attacks, as they are characterized by forces attacking in several locations in parallel. The lack of such abilities caused the Indian forces to respond slowly, be disorganized and experience command and control failures - leading to their failure of preventing the attackers from entrenching themselves and carrying out their plans. Unlike the Indian police, the attackers maintained good communication and coordination, used diverse weaponry (light weapons, explosives, etc.), and moved from one location to another - further confusing the already inferior Indian policemen. 


A West Point analysis suggested that in order to respond successfully, India would have to create regional SWAT units capable of instant deployment, as opposed to waiting hours for the national tactical team to arrive, as was the case during the Mumbai attack, or at least by a local police force trained for urban counterterrorism or counterinsurgency. Additionally, such a force requires high situational awareness and active intelligence support - both were lacking. Moreover, security in most soft targets was weak at best, and there were no plans as to how to deal with medical, hostage release, and other aspects of attack response requiring immediate attention, or with the media that had a double effect - increasing chaos and supplying the attackers with information (Sullivan & Elkus, 2009). 


While the Israeli forces also suffered from uncoordinated intelligence flow and from command and control issues, they performed much better than the Indian forces, while dealing with a much more complicated event with thousands of attackers. First, Israel has local SWAT teams (Lotar), as well as emergency response teams trained in counterterrorism and urban warfare and armed civilians who performed military service, on the ground at any given moment. These forces, alongside similar forces who self-joined from other regions, were able to delay and even halt some of the attackers, without waiting for massive forces to arrive from the headquarters. Second, despite intelligence gaps, the forces on the ground maintained high situational awareness. Even if the collected intelligence did not make its way to their more senior commanders, it allowed the responding forces to tailor their response to the situation and respond properly. Lastly, even soft targets like civilian communities are relatively well guarded by IDF bases positioned in close proximity, and there are elaborate plans pertaining to all aspects of a terrorism attack, including hostage release, medical treatment, and the media. Albeit not working perfectly on October 7th, 2023, these plans served as a basis to the response, delegating responsibilities between different forces. 


There are several important things to consider when conducting such a research. First, in this paper, we have used fallen security personnel as proxy for fighting forces, as there were thousands of people who self-joined, many of them undocumented. Albeit unlikely in a scale that would significantly alter the results, there is a possibility of a survivor bias, or in other words, that those who survived carry different characteristics from those who perished. Second, the exact time of death is not always known, although efforts were made to verify it to the largest extent possible by cross-referencing. 


Lastly, while the sample of N=369 is large enough to perform individual analysis, it is too small to perform location-based analysis. This is an endeavour reserved for future research. Another avenue for future research engages with the determinants of the time passed until operational control. When embarking on such research, it is important to note that the standard deviation of time elapsed until operation control between locations is significant, as certain locations, like Kfar Aza, were only retaken after nearly 3 days, while other locations saw the terrorists leaving after only a few minutes. Furthermore, certain locations saw an elaborate response of various forces, while in other locations, no security personnel were present. 


Finally, analyzing the battles of October 7th, 2023, leads to the inevitable conclusion that while technology, infrastructure, intelligence, and hierarchy all failed, the individuals succeeded. The ERC model, enabled by private initiative, shared language and skills, and decentralization prevented a larger catastrophe. While it is possible it would have helped to deploy more combat soldiers at the Gaza Envelope on the eve of the attack, it is also a telling that thousands of people, more than 40% of responders elected, on their own free will, to join the fighting and protect their country and fellow Israelis. This should serve as a lesson to Israel and to other countries facing swarm terrorism, security challenges more broadly - it is worth developing embedded response capacity by maintaining reserve units which are de-facto decentralized resilience mechanisms, training relevant individuals to respond to security crises independently, supplying local forces with weaponry that allows them to respond effectively against commando and swarming forces, instill in internal security forces that understanding of military-style scenarios and their role in them, and most importantly - developing an ethos of responsibility and joining when needed.

Two important factors set the stage for the development of such crisis-ready response teams. First, the primacy of the low-intensity threat Israel faces on a daily basis necessitates it to train masses of combat soldiers able to perform as both riot police and counterterrorism (to respond to Palestinian uprisings and terrorist attacks), and as specialized fighters able to fight in the less common case of a conventional war. The fact that Israel follows a mandatory military service means the IDF, and to a certain degree other security agencies, are able to select the most suitable candidates for these positions. The revered status of a combat soldier, let alone a special operations soldier, in Israel enlarges the pool of talented young recruits at the IDF's disposal, many of whom extend their service as a precondition to serving in certain roles, allowing for more specialized training. After they are discharged, they serve as reservists, maintaining some of their operational capabilities and the IDF’s ability to utilize them in times of crisis and to maintain operational knowledge. Moreover, most of those recruited to the Police tactical units and the Shin Bet operational units have served in combat positions in the IDF, further enhancing their specialization and combat and crisis response capabilities (Ben-Ari et al., 2023). These conditions create a latent army that can be activated at any given moment.
